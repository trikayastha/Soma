The tradition

A month measured
by the moon.

In the Hindu calendar, the month follows the moon: waxing from new to full (the Shukla Paksha), then waning back to dark (the Krishna Paksha). Each lunar day, or tithi, has its own character, and a few of them have long been set aside for vrata — the vow to fast, reflect, and eat lightly.

These fasts were never about going without for its own sake. They’re a standing appointment with yourself: a way to let the stomach rest, the mind settle, and the day slow down. All Soma would do is help me keep that appointment when life gets busy.

The fasts it would follow

Four turnings
of the month.

  • एकादशी

    Ekadashi

    The eleventh day of each fortnight, so twice a month. The most widely kept fast, traditionally observed without grains.

  • पूर्णिमा

    Purnima

    The full moon. A night of fullness and offering, when the month tips over and begins to wane.

  • अमावस्या

    Amavasya

    The new moon. The dark, quiet turn of the month — a time for stillness, remembrance, and starting again.

  • प्रदोष

    Pradosh

    The thirteenth tithi, kept at dusk. A short evening fast in the soft hour between day and night.
