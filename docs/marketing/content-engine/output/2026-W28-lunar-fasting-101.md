> **1/** Before there were fasting apps, there was the sky.
> In the Hindu calendar the month doesn't run on dates — it runs on the moon. And four lunar days ask you to fast.
> Here's what each one is, and how to keep it. ↓

> **2/** The unit isn't the date. It's the *tithi* — the lunar day. Each carries its own energy, and on certain ones you meet it with a fast, a *vrat*.
> Because they track the moon and not the calendar, the day shifts a little every month. That's the part that trips people up.

> **3/** Start with **Ekadashi** (एकादशी) — the 11th lunar day of each half of the cycle, so it comes twice a month.
> Traditionally kept *without grains*: no rice, wheat or lentils. Many take fruit or milk instead. A lighter body, a quieter mind, for a day.
> [attach: waxing-crescent moon image]

> **4/** Then the two poles of the month.
> **Purnima** (पूर्णिमा) — the full moon. Fullness, offering, gratitude.
> **Amavasya** (अमावस्या) — the new moon. Stillness, rest, starting over.
> One is a peak. The other is a reset.

> **5/** "Do I have to give up all food?"
> No. How strictly you keep a fast is yours to choose — fruit, milk, or one simple meal are all common.
> The intention was never punishment. It's rhythm.

> **6/** There's a fourth: **Pradosh** (प्रदोष) — the 13th day, kept in the evening twilight. Short, twice a month.
> Together the four give the month a shape: fast, feast, rest, begin again.

> **7/** The catch: the tithi shifts monthly, so the date is never fixed.
> Soma computes tonight's tithi and shows your next fast — free.
> Bookmark this; the next Ekadashi will sneak up on you.
