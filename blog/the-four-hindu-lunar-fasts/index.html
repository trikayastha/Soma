Lunar Fasting 101 · 8 July 2026

The four main Hindu lunar fasts, explained

In the Hindu calendar, the month runs with the moon — and certain lunar days ask you to fast. Here are the four you’ll meet most often, and how to keep them.

Long before calendars and clocks, the sky told you when to fast, when to rest, and when to begin again. In Hindu practice — Sanatan Dharma, the eternal way — the month doesn’t run on fixed dates. It runs on the moon. It grows from the new moon to the full, then fades back into darkness, and every turn begins a new cycle.

Each lunar day is called a tithi, and each carries its own energy. On certain tithis we meet that energy with a fast — a vrat — bringing the body into synergy with the sky and giving it a moment to reset. Because these days follow the moon and not the calendar, the date shifts a little each month. That’s the one thing that trips up beginners, and it’s exactly the thing Soma works out for you.

Four fasts come up again and again. Start with these.

Ekadashi एकादशी

Ekadashi is the eleventh lunar day of each half of the lunar cycle, which means it comes twice a month — once as the moon waxes, once as it wanes. It’s the fast most people keep, and the one to begin with.

Traditionally it’s observed without grains — no rice, wheat, or lentils — with many people taking fruit, milk, or simple non-grain foods instead. How strictly you keep it is up to you; the intention is a lighter body and a quieter mind for a day.

Purnima पूर्णिमा

Purnima is the full moon — the peak of the month, right as it turns from growing to fading. It’s a day for offering and gratitude, and a natural high point to pause and take stock. Many keep a partial fast through the day and break it once the full moon has risen.

Amavasya अमावस्या

Amavasya is the new moon — the dark, quiet end of the month. Where Purnima is fullness, Amavasya is stillness: a good time to slow down, turn inward, remember those who came before, and start over. The fast here is gentler and more reflective.

Pradosh प्रदोष

Pradosh (or Pradosham) falls on the thirteenth lunar day, and unlike the others it’s kept in the evening — in the twilight window just before nightfall. It’s a short one, and like Ekadashi it comes twice a month.

How to start keeping the rhythm

You don’t have to memorise a calendar or do the lunar maths. The simplest way in:

  • Pick one fast. Ekadashi is the usual starting point — twice a month, widely kept, easy to explain.
  • Keep it simply. Skip grains for the day, eat lightly, drink water. You can always go deeper later.
  • Let the app tell you when. The tithi shifts each month, so the date isn’t fixed — Soma computes tonight’s tithi and shows you the next fast, so you never have to guess.

You don’t need to be Hindu, or an expert, to follow the moon. It’s a spiritual wellness practice that anyone can keep — one fast, one phase at a time.
